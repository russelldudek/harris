Interview thesis brief · AI Enablement Lead

Dispatch AI to the work.

Thesis: Run internal AI enablement like a field-service operation: capture friction, triage value and risk, dispatch the smallest useful intervention, verify it in real work, and reroute based on evidence.

1. Actual mandate

Stand up a one-person internal AI enablement function for ServiceLink that can assess readiness, build literacy, discover and prioritize cross-functional use cases, deliver practical AI solutions, govern use, coordinate boundaries with R&D and IT, build departmental champions, and report adoption, blockers, opportunities, and outcomes directly to the EVP.

2. Why the mandate is difficult

Breadth vs bandwidth

The role serves multiple departments without direct reports. A visible queue and champion system are required to create leverage.

Quick wins vs durable change

Early proof builds trust, but novelty is not adoption. The system must measure retained behavior and workflow outcomes.

Speed vs governance

Approved tools, data handling, human authority, and escalation need to be clear enough for everyday work.

Builder vs influencer

The lead must ship useful solutions while transferring ownership to department leaders, users, and champions.

Enablement vs shadow IT/R&D

Some requests should be built locally; others must be escalated to the accountable technical or product owner.

Demand vs value

AI interest will exceed capacity. Prioritization must make tradeoffs explicit and defensible.

5. Intake and prioritization

Value

What business outcome or workflow friction changes, and how will we know?

Feasibility

Can the intervention be delivered with current tools, skills, data, and access?

Data + trust

What information is used, what is sensitive, and where must human authority remain?

Adoption burden

How much behavior, role, process, or manager support must change?

Owner strength

Is there a department leader and workflow owner prepared to sustain the change?

Reuse

Is the value local, or can a successful pattern help another workflow or department?

8. Hard objection

“Will a current Director of Operations thrive as a hands-on IC with no direct reports?”

Response: The strongest through-line is mechanism-building, not hierarchy: observe the workflow, make the problem visible, build or shape the intervention, connect technical and business people, teach the new standard, instrument the result, and iterate. Position the lack of a team as a reason to build leverage through a queue, champions, standards, reusable interventions, and explicit boundaries—not as a gap to hide.

  1. What does unequivocal first-year success look like to the EVP?

  3. What are the current approved-tool and data-handling boundaries?
  4. When should this role build, and when should work move to IT or R&D?
  5. Which workflows are already painful enough that leaders are asking for help?
  6. What made prior cross-functional change stick—or fade?
