The real mandate

Build the function while doing the work.

The role combines readiness assessment, training, use-case discovery, backlog management, lightweight business cases, hands-on delivery, governance, executive reporting, and change management. The trap is obvious: one person can become a workshop calendar, a random-request queue, or shadow IT. The mandate is to create an operating system that makes limited enablement capacity compound.

Operating tensions

The role lives in the seams.

The JD is unusually clear about boundaries, but the daily work will still happen between them. These are the contradictions the operating model has to manage—not eliminate.

Breadth ↔ bandwidth

Serve the enterprise without becoming the bottleneck.

Use a visible queue, explicit routing rules, and departmental champions to multiply one-person capacity.

Quick wins ↔ durable change

Win trust, then change behavior.

A quick win is useful only if it becomes repeatable work, not a one-time demo.

Speed ↔ governance

Guardrails must travel with the work.

Approved tools, data handling, human authority, and escalation should be clear before adoption spreads.

Builder ↔ influencer

Build enough to earn credibility.

The role must ship useful interventions and teach leaders and users how to own the changed workflow.

Enablement ↔ shadow IT

Know when to route the work away.

Not every request should be built locally. Some belong with IT, R&D, policy owners, or no one at all.

Candidate thesis
Run AI enablement like a field-service operation.

ServiceLink’s own domain is about getting the right resource to the right work with scheduling, optimization, routing, dispatch, visibility, and feedback. My hypothesis is that internal AI enablement should use the same operating logic.

Capture the friction. Triage value, readiness, data risk, and adoption burden. Dispatch the smallest useful intervention. Verify it in real work. Reroute based on evidence.

The role then becomes more than a knowledgeable AI person. It becomes a transparent mechanism for turning business pain into governed, adopted, measurable improvement.

Dispatch decision architecture

Five routes. One visible queue.

The first decision is not “which AI tool?” It is “what kind of intervention does this work actually need?” The route determines effort, ownership, governance, and measurement.

01 / ENABLE

Teach the work.

Role-specific training, examples, office hours, playbooks, and champion coaching when the blocker is literacy or confidence.

Do not build software for a learning problem.
02 / ASSIST

Augment the worker.

Copilots, prompts, templates, knowledge retrieval, summarization, drafting, and structured review aids.

Human judgment remains explicit.
03 / AUTOMATE

Remove repeatable friction.

Low-code workflows, routing, notifications, classification, data movement, and bounded task orchestration.

Instrument exceptions and failure modes.
04 / BUILD + INTEGRATE

Create a local tool.

Internal bots, lightweight applications, scripts, integrations, and APIs when workflow value justifies deeper delivery.

Coordinate technical boundaries with IT and R&D.
05 / ESCALATE

Route it away.

Send infrastructure, product-roadmap, architecture, identity, access, or high-risk decisions to the accountable owner.

Good enablement includes saying “not here.”

First 90 days

Learn the routes before optimizing them.

The first quarter should create evidence, trust, and a repeatable operating cadence—not a giant AI transformation program.

Days 0–30 · Map

Observe the real work.

  • Interview department heads and frontline users.
  • Assess readiness by function, role, approved tools, data sensitivity, and manager sponsorship.
  • Inventory current AI usage, shadow practices, training needs, and existing policies.
  • Establish intake, scoring, route definitions, and baseline measures.
  • Identify potential departmental champions.
Days 31–60 · Dispatch

Ship bounded quick wins.

  • Run a small cross-functional set of field tests across different dispatch routes.
  • Pair each use case with a business owner, baseline, human authority, and success measure.
  • Deliver role-specific workshops tied to live workflows, not generic AI literacy.
  • Publish lightweight approved-use and data-handling guidance with the right owners.
  • Begin EVP reporting on queue, flow, adoption, blockers, and outcomes.
Days 61–90 · Close the loop

Turn wins into a system.

  • Retire, reroute, or iterate weak experiments based on evidence.
  • Stand up champion cadence and reusable playbooks by workflow pattern.
  • Formalize backlog review and R&D/IT escalation boundaries.
  • Instrument retained adoption and workflow-specific outcomes.
  • Present the next-quarter portfolio with explicit capacity tradeoffs.
“You are already a Director of Operations. Will you really thrive as a hands-on individual contributor with no direct reports?”

The concern is fair. My answer is that the part of leadership I value most is not headcount—it is making work better. My strongest work has repeatedly been close to the mechanism: observe the workflow, make the problem visible, build or shape the intervention, connect technical and business people, teach the new standard, instrument the result, and keep iterating. This role is attractive precisely because it combines those activities in one mandate. The first version of ServiceLink’s AI enablement function needs leverage before hierarchy, and I would treat that as a feature of the role, not a temporary inconvenience.

Executive interview questions

Questions that expose the real constraint.

The answers would determine where the Dispatch system should start and what it should deliberately not do.

What would make the EVP say this role was unequivocally successful after twelve months: capacity, speed, quality, customer experience, revenue support, risk reduction, or a specific combination?
Where is AI already being used informally across ServiceLink, and which department has the largest gap between enthusiasm and reliable workflow adoption?
What is the current approved-tool and data-handling boundary, and where is guidance still ambiguous to employees?
When a business use case needs more than a prompt or low-code automation, what is the preferred boundary between this role, IT, and R&D?
Which workflows are painful enough that department heads already ask for help, and which requests look attractive but are strategically low value?
How are successful internal practices shared across ServiceLink today, and where does knowledge transfer tend to break down?
What level of hands-on building do you expect from this role versus enabling business users, configuring existing tools, or coordinating deeper technical work?
What has made previous cross-functional change efforts stick here—and what has caused them to fade after launch?
