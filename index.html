The company moment

Scale is not the hard part. Transfer is.

Harris publicly describes itself as decentralized yet aligned. That is a strength—and the defining AI enablement design constraint. The role should not pull judgment away from business units. It should make better local judgment easier, safer, measurable, and reusable.

Autonomy ↔ reuse

Preserve the edge.

Standardize the transfer packet, not every local workflow decision.

Access ↔ redesign

Beyond licenses.

Tool availability is not adoption. Rework the job, decision, handoff, and review boundary.

Speed ↔ trust

Move quickly, visibly.

Make data class, human authority, failure modes, and escalation explicit before scale.

Platform ↔ vertical nuance

Share primitives.

Reuse architecture and learning while allowing domain language, controls, and workflows to adapt.

Activity ↔ value

Count changed work.

Measure retained behavior and business outcomes, not prompt volume or demo theater.

Candidate thesis
AI enablement should compound.

My hypothesis: Harris can accelerate MatchaAI adoption by treating each successful local workflow as a potential reusable asset—not a one-off win.

The central enablement team creates the transfer standard, coaching system, governance rails, instrumentation, and learning commons. Business units retain authority over which work matters, what local adaptation is required, and whether the outcome is real.

The result is not central control. It is faster local value with a shorter path from someone proved it to we can adapt it safely here.

First 90 days

Listen for transfer friction. Then prove the exchange.

The first quarter should produce evidence, not an empire. Begin by learning how Harris businesses already discover, build, govern, teach, and measure AI-enabled work. Then test the Mission Passport on a small set of willing teams.

Days 0–30

Map the real system

  • Interview platform, security, group, portfolio, and BU leaders.
  • Shadow real workflows and current enablement moments.
  • Inventory existing Missions, adoption signals, training, and governance touchpoints.
  • Identify where good work currently stalls: discovery, proof, trust, transfer, or measurement.
Days 31–60

Run three transfer tests

  • Select a small set of proven or promising workflows with clear owners.
  • Create lightweight Mission Passports with baseline, controls, and adaptation notes.
  • Pair originating and receiving teams.
  • Measure time-to-understand, time-to-adapt, adoption friction, and outcome evidence.
Days 61–90

Turn learning into rails

  • Publish a v1 enablement exchange playbook.
  • Recommend which elements should be shared standards versus local choices.
  • Stand up a balanced scorecard and visible decision cadence.
  • Prioritize the next cohort based on value, readiness, transferability, and trust burden.
“You have not already done this exact job at this exact scale. Why you?”

That is the right objection. My answer is not to disguise the gap. It is to show the through-line: I have led operational change where software, hardware, people, customer consequence, and imperfect information meet; built AI-readiness and agentic workflow models; run 24/7 operating mechanisms; and translated emerging technology across technical and business stakeholders. The transfer claim is practical: the work of AI enablement is not primarily tool promotion. It is workflow selection, adoption design, governance, operating cadence, learning transfer, and value realization. Those are the muscles I would bring—and I would enter Harris with enough humility to learn the local system before prescribing one.

Executive questions

What I would want to learn.

These questions are designed to reveal the actual enablement constraint before solving the wrong problem efficiently.

When a Mission succeeds in one business unit today, what prevents another unit from discovering and adapting it?
Which decisions are intentionally local, and which AI controls or standards should be common across the portfolio?
What would make leaders say this role created value after twelve months: adoption, productivity, customer impact, reuse, risk reduction, or something else?
Where does enablement currently break down most often: problem selection, data access, change burden, manager sponsorship, training, governance, or measurement?
What is the strongest internal argument against scaling AI enablement more aggressively, and what evidence would change that view?
