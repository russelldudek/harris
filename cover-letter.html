Dear Service-Link Hiring Team,

Service-Link is asking one person to do several jobs that companies often split apart: assess AI readiness, teach non-technical teams, discover and prioritize use cases, build and deploy solutions, establish guardrails, create internal momentum, and report progress and blockers directly to the EVP. The only way that mandate works is to build an operating system—not become a calendar of generic workshops or a queue of random “can AI do this?” requests.

My candidate thesis is to run internal AI enablement like a field-service operation. Service-Link’s own domain is getting the right resource to the right work through scheduling, optimization, routing, dispatch, visibility, and feedback. Internally, I would apply the same logic: capture workflow friction, triage value, feasibility, data risk, and adoption burden; dispatch the smallest useful intervention; verify it in real work; then reroute, iterate, standardize, or stop based on evidence.

That mandate closely matches the through-line of my career. As Director of Operations at Vape-Jet, I lead AI-first operating transformation across production, purchasing, quality, customer success, support, engineering issue flow, ERP/Odoo, Jira, HubSpot, Slack, telemetry, and AI-assisted workflows. The work is practical: connecting factory, field, customer, and machine-fleet signals to prioritized action, earlier risk detection, cleaner handoffs, dashboards, standard work, training, and accountability.

Through DudeWorth, I develop AI-readiness and opportunity frameworks around value, feasibility, data readiness, governance, adoption burden, reuse, and time-to-value, along with agentic workflow patterns that preserve human judgment, escalation, decision records, evaluation, and governance rails. Earlier, at Amazon, I built operating mechanisms in a 24/7 customer-backward environment. At Compunetics, I moved through R&D program management into operations and engineering leadership, translating complex technical work into quality systems, process controls, customer delivery, and disciplined execution.

I also want to address the obvious question directly. I am currently a Director of Operations, and this role has no direct reports. That does not reduce its appeal. The work I value most has always been close to the mechanism: observe the workflow, make the problem visible, build or shape the intervention, teach the new standard, instrument the result, and keep iterating. I am not looking for headcount as proof of impact. I am looking for a mandate where practical AI adoption is the work.

I would welcome the chance to discuss where AI use is already emerging across Service-Link, which workflows are painful enough to deserve immediate attention, and how the boundary between enablement, IT, and R&D should operate in practice.

Carpe diem,
Russell Dudek
