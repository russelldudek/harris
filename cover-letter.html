Dear Harris Hiring Team,

Harris has a rare AI enablement problem: not whether AI can be useful, but how useful work can travel across a portfolio of more than 200 businesses without erasing the local judgment that makes vertical-market software companies effective. Harris’s own public language captures the tension well: the organization is decentralized yet aligned, and it is accelerating MatchaAI to support security-first Missions and generative tools.

That is why the AI Enablement Lead role interests me. I believe the opportunity is to make AI adoption compound. A successful local workflow should be able to become a reusable, governed pattern that another business unit can discover, evaluate, adapt, and measure faster—while keeping authority close to the work. My candidate vision calls this the Mission Passport: a practical transfer standard containing workflow context, a responsible owner, baseline evidence, human authority, trust boundaries, value proof, dependencies, and adaptation learning.

My evidence is adjacent, operational, and deliberate. As Director of Operations at Vape-Jet, I lead AI-first operating transformation across production, purchasing, quality, customer success, support, engineering issue flow, ERP/Odoo, Jira, HubSpot, Slack, telemetry, and AI-assisted workflows. The work is not AI theater; it is connecting factory, field, customer, and machine-fleet signals to earlier risk detection, cleaner handoffs, standard work, training, and accountability.

Through DudeWorth, I have developed AI-readiness and opportunity frameworks around value, feasibility, data readiness, governance, adoption burden, reuse, and time-to-value, along with agentic workflow patterns that preserve human judgment, escalation, decision records, evaluation, and governance rails. Earlier, at Amazon, I built operating mechanisms in a 24/7 customer-backward environment, and at Compunetics I led engineering, manufacturing, quality, and technical programs in high-reliability settings. Across those roles, the through-line is consistent: make complex work visible, connect technical and business teams, and build mechanisms that turn emerging capability into adopted behavior and measurable outcomes.

I also want to name the obvious concern directly. I have not already led AI enablement inside a 15,000-person, 200-business software operating group. I would not pretend otherwise. What I bring is the operating muscle underneath the title: workflow selection, cross-functional translation, adoption design, governance, escalation, knowledge transfer, standard work, and value realization. I would enter Harris by learning the real system before prescribing one, then testing whether a lightweight transfer mechanism can shorten the path from local proof to safe reuse.

I would welcome the chance to discuss where AI enablement currently stalls at Harris, what a successful first year would change in daily work, and whether the Mission Passport hypothesis is worth testing against the reality of MatchaAI adoption.

Carpe diem,

Russell Dudek
